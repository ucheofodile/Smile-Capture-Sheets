When Home Is Not Your Sanctuary

Guest — Ama Karikari  ·  Series: Beneath the Smile, Episode 1  ·  Status: Recorded — in post-production

This is not about what happened to her.
It's about what she had to carry while succeeding.

Core Message
Spine
Ama Karikari spent fifteen years carrying something at home that nobody around her could name — while building a career, teaching communication, and running for public office. This episode asks a different question: what does it cost to keep performing at the highest level while carrying something nobody else can see? And what becomes possible when that weight is finally named.
What This Episode Is Not About
Not a relationship story. Not a divorce debate. Not a clinical conversation about abuse.

The risk is someone reducing this to: "woman talks about silent treatment in marriage." The episode is actually: "what happens when someone builds an extraordinary career while carrying something nobody else can see?" Every clip, caption, thumbnail and comment response must reflect the second framing, never the first.
Three Moments That Carry The Episode
Protect these equally in the edit, the clips, and the chapters.

The WhatsApp story — leadership requires attention; this is what stole it. Compartmentalisation is an illusion. Do not treat this as a side anecdote.

"Even neutrality is undermining" — support is infrastructure, not a feeling. Absence of harm is not the same as presence of support.

"The wrong section of the library" — she spent years searching in the right spirit and the wrong category. This is why so many capable women stay confused for years.
Audience Promise
Ama's story is the invitation. The audience's own story is the destination. Cut toward self-recognition, not sympathy.

Editorial Lens
Overall Instruction
We are not telling the story of a marriage. We are telling the story of what someone was carrying while everyone else thought she was fine.

Keep enough detail to understand what happened and why it mattered. Cut detail that only makes the story more graphic or more specific without making it more meaningful.

If you're ever choosing between preserving information and preserving emotion — preserve emotion.
Tone to Preserve
Two women talking — warm, direct, occasionally surprised by where the conversation goes. Not an interview. The host's own keeping-malice story and WhatsApp story must stay in — they are what make this a conversation rather than an interrogation. Ama's voice is articulate and passionate; do not cut her mid-thought to tighten time. Let her complete the arc before cutting.
Edit For Three Questions
1. What was going on behind the scenes?
2. Where did it start leaking into the rest of her life?
3. What might have been different if it wasn't there?

If a moment answers one of these, keep it. If it only adds detail about the incident itself, cut it.
Restart Markers
No "strawberry" restart markers were used in this recording. Single continuous session. No splice points needed beyond standard filler word removal.
Pacing Notes
Do not rush the opening. The first minute works because it sounds conversational, not produced. Give it breathing room even if other sections need to be trimmed to compensate.

The WhatsApp story is load-bearing. Do not trim it for time. It is the clearest proof in the episode that compartmentalising is an illusion — leadership requires attention, and this is what stole it.

The support section (Ketanji, neutrality, could-have-been) may feel like it belongs earlier. It doesn't. Let it arrive where it arrived in the conversation — after the cost has been fully established.

Episode Description
This is the first episode of Beneath the Smile — a new series within Don't Let the Smile Fool You for the conversations that go a little deeper. Ama Karikari spent fifteen years building a successful career, teaching communication, and running for public office, while carrying something at home that nobody around her could name. This is not an episode about a marriage. It's about what it costs a high-performing woman to keep functioning when nobody can see what she's carrying — and what becomes possible when it is finally named.
